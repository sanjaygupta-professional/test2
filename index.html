Example: Logistic Regression for credit card default prediction in banking

‚ùå

Weak Example

Logistic Regression is a machine learning algorithm used for classification. In our bank, we can use it to predict which customers will default on their credit cards. We will collect lots of data and train the model. If the accuracy is high, the model is good and we can use it to approve or reject customers. Logistic Regression is good because it is simple and used a lot in the industry. We will check the confusion matrix and try to reduce errors. This will help the bank reduce risk.

Issues:

  • Very generic; not tied to concrete business scenario
  • No specific mention of data variables or sources
  • Relies only on "accuracy" without discussing class imbalance
  • No explicit link to business KPIs
  • No discussion of regulatory or fairness considerations
  • No mention of validation approach or bias-variance
‚úì

Strong Example

Business problem: The bank wants to reduce credit card defaults in its mass retail segment without unnecessarily rejecting profitable customers. Binary classification: default within 12 months (yes/no).

Data: 3 years of historical data including demographics, account behavior (utilization ratio, late payments), and bureau variables. Target: 90+ days past due within 12 months.

Why Logistic Regression: Risk team and regulators require interpretable model with clear coefficients and odds ratios. Less prone to overfitting with proper regularization.

KPIs: Reduce default rate (NPL%) and expected loss while maintaining approval rates. Focus on recall/precision for 5-7% default class, not overall accuracy.

Validation: Train/validation/test split with stratified k-fold CV. L2 regularization controls variance. Track performance over time to detect drift.

Risks: Assumes linear log-odds relationship. Address with interaction terms and regular back-testing. Check for bias across protected groups for regulatory compliance.

Strengths:

  • Clearly states business problem and time horizon
  • Describes data in realistic detail
  • Explains algorithm choice with interpretability focus
  • Links to concrete business KPIs and discusses misclassification costs
  • Shows understanding of validation and overfitting control
  • Addresses governance, fairness, and regulatory aspects
